## Paper Overview

The methods used in CIFAR-10 Airbench are detailed in the research paper:

<Card title="CIFAR-10 Airbench: Fast Neural Network Training" icon="file-lines" href="https://arxiv.org/abs/2404.00498">
  Read the full paper on arXiv (arXiv:2404.00498)
</Card>

This paper presents a comprehensive set of techniques that enable training neural networks on CIFAR-10 to high accuracy in a matter of seconds rather than minutes.

## Methods

The paper describes several key techniques:

### 1. GPU-Accelerated Data Loading

Unlike traditional dataloaders that perform augmentation on CPU, Airbench moves all data processing to the GPU. This eliminates CPU-GPU transfer bottlenecks and enables faster iteration times.

### 2. Efficient Augmentation Pipeline

The augmentation strategy is carefully designed to provide regularization while maintaining training speed:
- Random horizontal flips
- Random translations
- Cutout regularization

### 3. Optimized Network Architecture

The network architecture is specifically designed for fast training while maintaining high accuracy, with careful attention to:
- Layer widths and depths
- Activation functions
- Normalization strategies

### 4. Training Schedule

Precisely tuned training schedules with:
- Warmup phases
- Learning rate decay
- Label smoothing
- Momentum scheduling

## Beyond the Paper

Since the paper's publication, two additional techniques have pushed the records even further:

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="Muon Optimizer">
    The current 94% accuracy record uses the [Muon optimizer](https://kellerjordan.github.io/posts/muon/), a novel optimization algorithm that improves convergence speed and training stability.
    
    **Key benefits:**
    - Faster convergence to target accuracy
    - More stable training dynamics
    - Better generalization
    
    **Performance:** Reduces training time from 3.09s to 2.59s (16% improvement)
  </Accordion>
  
  <Accordion title="Data Filtering">
    The `airbench96_faster.py` script incorporates sophisticated data filtering techniques that identify and prioritize the most informative training examples.
    
    **Key benefits:**
    - More efficient use of training time
    - Focus on hard examples
    - Reduced computational cost
    
    **Performance:** Reduces training time from 34.7s to 27.3s (21% improvement)
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

## Prior Work and Context

CIFAR-10 Airbench builds upon a rich history of research in fast neural network training:

### hlb-CIFAR10

<Card title="hlb-CIFAR10" icon="github" href="https://github.com/tysam-code/hlb-CIFAR10">
  Previous speedrun record: 6.3 A100-seconds to 94% accuracy
</Card>

The [hlb-CIFAR10 project](https://github.com/tysam-code/hlb-CIFAR10) by tysam-code established the previous speedrun record, achieving 94% accuracy in 6.3 seconds on an A100. Airbench improves upon this by more than 2x, reaching the same accuracy in just 2.59 seconds.

### Myrtle.ai How to Train Your ResNet

<Card title="How to Train Your ResNet" icon="blog" href="https://myrtle.ai/learn/how-to-train-your-resnet/">
  Classic fast training series by Myrtle.ai
</Card>

The foundational work by [myrtle.ai](https://myrtle.ai/learn/how-to-train-your-resnet/) demonstrated that CIFAR-10 could be trained to 94% accuracy in 26 V100-seconds (equivalent to approximately 8+ A100-seconds). This influential series of blog posts introduced many of the techniques that modern speedrun attempts build upon.

### Evolution of Records

The progression of speedrun records shows the rapid advancement in training efficiency:

1. **Myrtle.ai (2018)**: ~26 V100-seconds (~8+ A100-seconds)
2. **hlb-CIFAR10 (2023)**: 6.3 A100-seconds
3. **Airbench Paper (2024)**: 3.09 A100-seconds
4. **Airbench + Muon (Current)**: 2.59 A100-seconds

<Info>
  The records continue to evolve. The README notes that [even faster methods](https://github.com/hiverge/cifar10-speedrun/tree/main) have since been developed, demonstrating the ongoing innovation in this space.
</Info>
